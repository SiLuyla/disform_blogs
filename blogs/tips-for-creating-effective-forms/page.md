I have reviewed a lot of forms created by Discord server admins. Most of them have
the same problems. They ask too many questions, they ask the wrong questions, and
they look like they were thrown together in two minutes.

A good form respects the person filling it out. A bad form treats them like a data
source. Here is how to build forms that people actually complete.

## 1. Ask only what you actually need

Every question you add is a reason for someone to abandon the form. If you do not
have a specific use for the answer, do not ask the question.

Before adding a question, ask yourself: "What will I do with this information?" If
you cannot answer that, cut it.

A membership application probably needs their Discord tag and a reason for
joining. It probably does not need their age, location, favorite color, and a
paragraph about their hobbies. Unless those are relevant, leave them out.

## 2. Put easy questions first

Start with the simple stuff. Name, Discord tag, or whatever identifying
information you need. Let people build momentum before you hit them with the long
paragraph questions.

If the first question is "Write a 500 word essay about why you want to join," most
people will close the tab. Warm them up with a few easy clicks first.

## 3. Use multiple choice when you can

Open ended text questions give you rich answers but they are hard to compare and
hard to process. Multiple choice questions are faster to fill out and easier to
analyze.

If you only need to know someone's experience level, use beginner/intermediate/
expert instead of "Describe your experience." You get cleaner data and the person
finishes faster.

Save long text for cases where you genuinely need someone to explain something in
their own words.

## 4. Explain why you are asking

If you ask for an email address without context, people hesitate. If you say "We
use this to send you a confirmation" or "Only used for follow up questions about
your application," they trust you more.

A short parenthetical explanation next to a question increases completion rates
noticeably. It takes two seconds to write and saves you from lost responses.

## 5. Group related questions

If your form has sections, group similar questions together. A form about event
registration might have sections for personal info, dietary preferences, and
availability.

DisForm supports sections, so use them. A form that jumps randomly between topics
feels disorganized and frustrates people. A form that flows logically from one
section to the next feels professional and intentional.

## 6. Set expectations upfront

Tell people how long the form will take. "This form takes about 3 minutes" at the
top of the page reduces abandonment significantly.

Also tell them what happens after they submit. Do they get a confirmation? Will
you DM them? Will there be a follow up interview? When people know what to expect,
they are more likely to hit submit.

## 7. Respect their time with defaults

If 80% of your applicants are from the US, default the country selector to United
States. If most orders are for the standard size, default to that.

Every click you save is friction removed. Small defaults add up to a noticeably
faster experience.

## 8. Test your own form

Fill out your own form before you share it. Then do it again on mobile. Then ask a
friend to fill it out and tell you what was confusing.

You will catch broken logic, missing options, and confusing wording every single
time. It takes two minutes and saves you from looking unprofessional when the
first person to use your form runs into a bug.

## 9. Add a progress bar for long forms

If your form has more than five questions, show people how far along they are. It
is a small thing but it dramatically reduces abandonment.

People tolerate a 20 question form if they know they are on question 14. They
abandon a 5 question form if it feels like it might be 20. Progress indicators
manage that anxiety.

## 10. Say thank you properly

The submission confirmation screen is the last thing people see. Make it good.

A plain "Your response has been recorded" is fine technically. A warm message that
matches your server's tone, maybe with next steps and a link back to your Discord,
leaves people feeling good about having submitted.

DisForm lets you customize the confirmation message. Use that feature. It takes
ten seconds and it is the difference between "I filled out a form" and "I had a
good experience with that community."

## The pattern is simple

Short forms with clear questions, logical grouping, and respectful treatment of
the respondent's time will always perform better than long, meandering forms that
ask for everything including their shoe size.

Focus on what you actually need, make it easy to provide, and thank them when they
are done. That is the whole formula.
